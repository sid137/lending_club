Interest

The fundamental idea behind interest, is that the borrower of a loan is obligated to repay more than they initially borrowed. We can think of this intrinsically as due to several reasons:

  • The lender should be compensated for making an unnecessary use of their money and taking a risk on the borrower
  • The lender could have profited better by putting their money to use in other ways than lending it to the borrower
As a result, the interest is the compensation that the lender is given for having catered to the borrower

The goal of extending a loan with interst is to have a high amount of money at the end of the loan period. We essentially want to have our initial amount, plus sum yet to be determined sum

\begin{equation} Total = Principal + Interest \end{equation}

We have two main ways of managing how this reward is determined, we we refer to this as how the interest acrues

Simple Interest

The simplest way to derive an interest payment is to consider it as a repayment of a fraction \(f\) of the initial amount \(P_0\) that was borrowed.
\begin{equation} Interest = fP_o < P_0 \end{equation}
When we continually add on interest amounts of this form, we can visualize this as a fixed amount of the initial capital, working to earn money. Because the income earning portion never changes or grows, the total growth of capital remains linear. We can represent this mathematically as an Arithmetic Series, where each payment amount is a term an the sequence.
\begin{equation} S_n = P_0 + \sum_{k=1}^N a_k \\ \end{equation} \begin{equation} S_n = P_0 + Na = P_0 + NfP_0 \\ \end{equation} \begin{equation} S_n = P_0(1 + Nf) \end{equation}
Because each interest payment is a fixed amount \(a = fP_0\), the \(a_k\) terms are all constant, and we thus have a linear growth of capital until the \(N^{th}\) payment.
